# Section Structure Reference

The standard 6-section landing page. Most pages need exactly these. Don't add more unless conversion data justifies it.

---

## 1. Hero — Headline + Subheadline + Primary CTA

**Goal**: Within 5 seconds, the visitor knows *what it is*, *who it's for*, *why they should care*, and *what to do next*.

- **Headline**: ≤ 12 words. One promise. Outcome-led, not feature-led.
- **Subheadline**: 1–2 sentences bridging the headline to the visitor's situation. Names the audience explicitly when possible.
- **Primary CTA button**: action verb + benefit. "Get instant access" beats "Sign up".
- Optional: hero image / 30-second demo video / 3 logos for proof.

**Avoid**: company-centric headlines ("Welcome to X"); buzzword soup; vague promises ("Take your business to the next level").

---

## 2. Sub-section / Value Proposition (the "why")

**Goal**: Convince the right person they're in the right place.

- 3–5 benefit-led bullets.
- Each bullet uses FAB internally — feature, advantage, benefit folded into one sentence.
- Bad: "We have a prompt library."
- Good: "Get a tested prompt vault — clone it once, save 10+ hours every week."

If the offer has 3 distinct pillars (e.g. *content*, *community*, *coaching*), structure this section as 3 mini-blocks with one clear pillar per block.

**Length**: 100–250 words.

---

## 3. Social Proof (the trust)

**Goal**: Reduce perceived risk before the price reveal.

Pick whichever you actually have:
- Member count: "Join 500+ founders already inside."
- Testimonials with name + role + (ideally) photo + measurable outcome.
- Case studies: before/after with numbers.
- Logo strip: "As featured in / Used by teams at".
- Press mentions.

If real proof is missing, mark `[PLACEHOLDER]` clearly. Never invent testimonials.

---

## 4. Mechanism / How It Works (optional but powerful)

**Goal**: Show *how* the result is achieved so the promise feels credible.

- 3 numbered steps. Each one sentence + a phrase explaining why it works.
- Example: "1. Pick a workflow. 2. Plug in your stack. 3. Ship in 24 hours."

This section converts skeptics. Skip it only if the mechanism is genuinely obvious.

---

## 5. Call to Action (the close)

**Goal**: Make the click frictionless.

- One CTA, repeated 2–3 times down the page.
- Restate the core benefit next to the button.
- Add scarcity *only if it's true*: "Cohort opens Jan 15", "47 of 100 seats left". Fake scarcity destroys trust.
- Show price + what's included immediately above the button.
- Risk reversal under the button: guarantee, cancel-anytime, etc.

---

## 6. FAQ (overcoming objections)

**Goal**: Answer the "yeah, but…" thoughts without making the visitor leave.

- 4–7 questions max. More than that signals you didn't address them in the body copy.
- Always cover: time commitment, skill level, pricing model, refund policy.
- Phrase as the visitor would type into Google: "Do I need to know how to code?" not "Coding requirements".
- Answers are 1–3 sentences. If you need a paragraph, the body copy is doing the wrong job.

---

## Optional sections (use sparingly)

- **Founder story** — only if your face/credibility is the offer.
- **Comparison table** vs alternatives — strong when solution-aware audience is comparing.
- **Pricing table** — only if you have multiple tiers; otherwise embed price in the CTA section.
- **Stack of bonuses** — works for info products at $200+ price points.

---

## Length targets (desktop view)

| Offer type | Total page length |
|------------|--------------------|
| $7–$47 digital product | 1 short scroll (~600 words) |
| $97–$297 community / course | 2 scrolls (~1200 words) |
| $1k+ cohort or coaching | 4–6 scrolls, includes founder story + detailed mechanism |
| Free lead magnet | ½ scroll, single section |

Going longer than this signals the offer isn't tight enough; going shorter signals you skipped objections.
